Every January, the world's most respected travel publications release their annual "where to go" lists. National Geographic, Lonely Planet, Conde Nast Traveller, AFAR, CNN — each brings their own lens, their own biases, their own definition of what makes a destination worth visiting right now.

The problem is not a lack of inspiration. It is the opposite. There are too many lists, scattered across too many websites, and by the time you have read four of them you cannot remember which destination appeared where or why it was recommended.

So we collected them all. Every major "where to travel in 2026" guide we could find, in one place, organised by category. Read the ones that match your travel style, ignore the rest, and save whatever catches your eye.

## The authoritative lists

These are the publications whose annual destination lists shape where millions of people travel. They are worth reading not just for the picks themselves but for the reasoning behind them — each publication has a distinct editorial philosophy.

**[Best of the World 2026](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/travel/article/best-of-the-world-2026)** — *nationalgeographic.com*
National Geographic's list carries perhaps the most prestige. Their picks tend to emphasise conservation, cultural significance, and the kind of travel that leaves a place better than you found it. This year's selection spans all seven continents — yes, including Antarctica.

**[Best in Travel 2026](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/best-in-travel-2026)** — *lonelyplanet.com*
Lonely Planet's annual flagship. Divided into countries, cities, and regions, with a focus on value, emerging destinations, and places where the travel infrastructure is improving. Their picks often become self-fulfilling prophecies — if Lonely Planet says go, people go.

**[The Best Places to Go in 2026](https://www.cntraveler.com/gallery/best-places-to-go)** — *cntraveler.com*
Conde Nast Traveller brings the luxury lens. These are not necessarily expensive destinations, but they are places where the experience has been thought through — design hotels, exceptional dining, curated cultural experiences. The publication for travellers who want quality over quantity.

**[Where to Go in 2026](https://www.afar.com/magazine/where-to-go-in-2026)** — *afar.com*
AFAR's list is the most philosophical. They do not just tell you where to go — they tell you why going matters, what you will learn, and how a place might change you. Their picks tend towards the culturally rich and personally transformative.

**[Where to Travel in 2026: The Best Destinations](https://edition.cnn.com/travel/where-to-travel-2026/)** — *cnn.com*
CNN's travel team casts a wide net — their list is longer and more varied than most, covering everything from city breaks to wilderness adventures. A good starting point if you genuinely have no idea where you want to go and want the broadest possible set of options.

**[Travel's Best Trips 2026](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/travel/article/best-trips-2026)** — *nationalgeographic.com*
A companion to NatGeo's "Best of the World" list, but focused on specific trips rather than destinations. Each entry includes a suggested itinerary or experience — more actionable than a destination recommendation alone.

## Food-focused destinations

For travellers who plan trips around what they will eat, not just where they will sleep.

**[The Best Places to Travel in 2026 for Food Lovers: 16 Must-Visit Destinations](https://guide.michelin.com/en/article/travel/best-places-to-travel-in-2026-for-food-lovers)** — *guide.michelin.com*
Michelin's food destination list draws from their unmatched network of inspectors and critics. These are cities and regions where the dining scene is not just good but genuinely world-leading — from traditional markets to Michelin-starred innovation.

**[The World's Best Food Destinations For 2026 Travel](https://www.timeout.com/travel/best-food-destinations)** — *timeout.com*
Timeout's take is more street-level and democratic than Michelin's. Great for travellers who would rather eat at a hawker centre than a tasting menu — though there is room for both.

## Family travel

Travelling with children changes the calculation entirely. These guides are written by people who understand that.

**[Best Family-Friendly Destinations 2026](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/best-family-travel-destinations-2026)** — *lonelyplanet.com*
Lonely Planet's family list goes beyond the obvious theme-park-and-resort picks. These are destinations where children will be welcomed, where logistics are manageable, and where the experiences are genuinely enriching for the whole family.

**[The Best Family Holiday Destinations for 2026](https://www.timeout.com/travel/best-family-holiday-destinations)** — *timeout.com*
A practical, been-there-done-it guide. Timeout's writers tend to be honest about the realities of travelling with children — including the things that do not work, not just the things that do.

## Europe-specific

If you already know you are staying on this continent, these guides drill deeper.

**[Where to Go in Europe in 2026](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/best-places-to-visit-in-europe)** — *lonelyplanet.com*
Lonely Planet's European picks for the year. A mix of established favourites and emerging destinations, with a particular eye on places where the exchange rate is favourable or new transport links have opened up.

**[15 Underrated European Destinations to Visit in 2026](https://www.timeout.com/travel/underrated-european-destinations)** — *timeout.com*
The counterpoint to every mainstream list. These are the European destinations that are not on everyone's radar yet — quieter, cheaper, and often more rewarding than the famous alternatives an hour down the road.

## Budget and accessibility

Great travel should not require a great fortune.

**[Most Affordable Destinations for 2026](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/best-value-destinations-2026)** — *lonelyplanet.com*
Lonely Planet's value picks. These are not just cheap destinations — they are places where your money buys exceptional experiences. There is a difference between "affordable" and "worth it," and this list focuses on the intersection.

## The broader picture

For the travellers who want to understand the trends shaping where people go and why.

**[Best Places to Visit in 2026: 50 Destinations We're Excited About](https://www.timeout.com/travel/best-places-to-visit)** — *timeout.com*
Fifty picks is ambitious, and Timeout rises to it. This is not a ranked list but a curated directory — browsing it feels like flipping through a beautifully edited travel magazine. Good for a Sunday afternoon with a cup of tea and a blank calendar.

**[Where to Go on Holiday in 2026: Travel Hotspots from Luxury Escapes to Budget Breaks](https://www.theguardian.com/travel/2025/dec/28/where-to-go-on-holiday-in-2026)** — *theguardian.com*
The Guardian brings British editorial sensibility to the genre — opinionated, slightly contrarian, and unafraid to recommend places that are not conventionally glamorous. Their "budget breaks" section is consistently excellent.

**[The Best New Hotels of 2026](https://www.cntraveler.com/gallery/best-new-hotels-in-the-world)** — *cntraveler.com*
Sometimes the destination is the hotel itself. Conde Nast Traveller's annual new-hotel round-up covers openings across every price point and style, from eco-lodges to urban design hotels. Worth reading even if you never book one — it tells you where the travel industry thinks people are heading.
