Tbilisi has one of the stranger spring weeks any European capital has ever staged. Over ten days between 1 and 10 May 2026, the city hosts the back-to-back Zero Compromise Natural Wine Festival, the biggest New Wine Festival in the country, and — for the first time ever — a new national public holiday called the **Day of Georgian Wine**. Georgia has been making wine for 8,000 years, longer than any other culture on earth. In 2026, it finally has a day on the calendar that says so.

It is also, frankly, the best week to visit. Orthodox Easter has passed. The wisteria is heavy over the old town balconies. The Alazani Valley is green and the Kakheti winemakers have their cellar doors propped open. The temperature sits comfortably between 15 and 25°C. And the tourist infrastructure that is arriving fast in Tbilisi — the Design Hotels conversions, the natural wine bars, the Michelin Guide selection — is all close enough to new that you can still feel like an early visitor without being a pioneer.

## Old Town in an afternoon

Tbilisi's old town — Kala, running up the hillside from the Mtkvari River — is a labyrinth of 19th-century wooden balconies, brick caravanserais, sulphur-tiled bathhouses and churches that go back to the fourth century. The emblematic set piece is **Abanotubani**, the sulphur bath district, where domed bath-houses squat under the rocky outcrop of the fortress hill; a private room for two at one of the more atmospheric baths (Chreli Abano is the photographable one with the Persian-tiled façade) costs around €15–30 for an hour. Above the river, the cable car from Rike Park swings you up to the Mother of Georgia monument in four minutes, and the panoramic balcony gives you the city's geography in a single look.

For context on how these pieces fit together, [this local springtime overview](https://friendly.ge/en/spring-tours-georgia) is the Georgian-operator view of what is actually happening in April and May, including the Orthodox Easter pilgrimages that give the mountain monasteries an atmosphere they do not have in high summer.

## Eating your way through a supra

Georgian food is one of the genuinely underrated cuisines on the planet, and Tbilisi is the best place to understand why. [Lonely Planet's primer on Georgian food](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/best-food-guide-georgia) is the essential background — the khinkali (Georgia's soup dumplings), the dozens of regional khachapuri variants (cheese breads, most famously the boat-shaped Adjaruli with a raw egg yolk cracked over the top), the chakapuli lamb-and-tarragon stew that is only served in spring, the fragrant nigvziani badrijani walnut-stuffed aubergine rolls, and the **supra** — the ritualised Georgian feast, led by a tamada (toastmaster) who delivers elaborate speeches to the table.

For actual addresses, [Wander-Lush's 50 best restaurants in Tbilisi for 2026](https://wander-lush.org/tbilisi-georgia-food-best-restaurants-cafes-guide/) is the definitive city food guide — organised by cuisine and neighbourhood, regularly updated, and honest about where locals actually eat versus where tourists are sent. [Red Fedora Diary's locally written 27-strong list](https://www.redfedoradiary.com/best-restaurants-in-tbilisi-georgian-food/) adds the dining rooms that Wander-Lush leaves out. And for the single best dumpling crawl in the city, [this five-restaurant khinkali tour](https://www.lydiatravels.com/2024/01/best-khinkali-in-tbilisi-georgia.html) is the one worth following to the letter — Cafe Daphna's individually ordered khinkali is a particular win for solo travellers.

## The wine calendar, May 2026

If you time one trip to Tbilisi around anything, time it around wine. [Red Fedora Diary's Georgian wine festivals guide](https://www.redfedoradiary.com/georgian-wine-festivals-practical-tips/) is the best single overview of the full calendar, and [Eat This Tours' running events list](https://eatthistours.com/georgian-food-wine-festivals-events-calendar-tbilisi/) is the one to keep tab-open for confirmed dates.

The key dates for 2026:

- **Zero Compromise Natural Wine Festival, 1–2 May**, at Radio City in Tbilisi — a two-day ticketed tasting with the 120+ producers of the Natural Wine Association. 30 GEL (around €10) gets you an all-you-can-drink two-day pass and access to the tamadas, artisans and small-producer importers that are the reason Georgia is the single most interesting wine country for natural-wine drinkers right now.
- **National Day of Georgian Wine, 8 May** — a brand-new public holiday, first observed in 2026, honouring 8,000 years of qvevri winemaking. Expect free tastings across the old town, producer pop-ups on Agmashenebeli Avenue, and a city that feels like it has collectively taken the afternoon off.
- **New Wine Festival, 9 May**, at Mtatsminda Park — an outdoor festival at the top of the funicular, free to enter, celebrating the previous autumn's vintage with live folk music and tastings from every major wine region.

For the operator view on how to fold all of this into a longer trip, [Gourmet Wine Travel's 2026 Georgia tour](https://gourmetwinetravel.com/georgian-wine-tasting-tour-2026/) is a useful reference even if you travel independently — it shows you which cellars and which towns belong on a serious wine week.

## Where to stay

Tbilisi's boutique hotel scene has arrived in the last five years. [Design Hotels' Tbilisi portfolio](https://www.designhotels.com/hotels/georgia/tbilisi/) includes The Blue Fox — a 17th-century merchant's mansion in the old town with murals by Musya Qeburia and a Michelin-starred restaurant — and Stamba Hotel, a former Soviet printing house converted into one of the most visually striking hotels in the Caucasus. For a wider selection, [Wander-Lush's 12 boutique picks for 2026](https://wander-lush.org/best-boutique-hotels-in-tbilisi/) goes neighbourhood by neighbourhood — Sololaki for the Art Nouveau streets, Vera for the cafés, old town Kala for the atmosphere. For a single detailed write-up of one of the standout conversions, [The House Hotel Old Tbilisi](https://www.myboutiquehotel.com/en/boutique-hotels-tbilisi-city/the-house-hotel-old-tbilisi.html) sits on the Mtkvari river at the edge of the Abanotubani bath district.

## The Kakheti day trip is the one worth doing

Kakheti, two hours east of Tbilisi, is where most of Georgia's wine comes from and where qvevri winemaking — the underground egg-shaped clay vessel method — is still living heritage rather than museum piece. [Wander-Lush's Kakheti itinerary](https://wander-lush.org/things-to-do-in-sighnaghi-telavi-kakheti-itinerary/) is the reference for planning — it covers Sighnaghi (the walled "city of love" on the ridge above the Alazani Valley), Telavi, Alaverdi Monastery's working 11th-century cellar, and the small-producer visits that make the day. [A compact day-trip account](https://journeyswithlejune.com/kakheti-a-day-trip-to-georgias-famous-wine-region/) is the best honest read on what fits into one day and what does not — the short answer is that Sighnaghi plus one cellar is the realistic limit, and two nights in Kakheti is better than one day.

## One week, eight thousand years

Georgia is the country that was quietly making wine while everyone else was inventing agriculture. For one week in May 2026 — starting with Zero Compromise on the 1st and closing with the New Wine Festival on the 9th — it is also the best argument in Europe for booking a shoulder-season flight into somewhere you have never been.
